## Data analysis notebooks for COMM3180 Group Data Project

This section walks through the analysis we conducted across three aspects: overall school comparisons, business schools, and engineering schools. Each folder contains notebooks, which are step-by-step documents, that combine our code, charts, and written explanations so that anyone can follow our reasoning and see how we arrived at our conclusions.

**Overall Folder:** 
Using the five datasets from the department of education and tax records, the analysis compared Penn and UIUC across financial aspects. The quantitative analysis in the notebook "overall_analysis.csv" examines median earnings at 6 and 10 years post-graduation, return on investment, median debt, debt-to-income ratio, and economic mobility data, providing a comparative visual for all of these aspects. The qualitative analysis in the notebook "sentiment_analysis.csv" includes a sentiment analysis across 50 sources (Reddit, Niche reviews, Alumni Surveys, Students Newspapers) to score each school on the overall undergraduate experience: quality of life, belonging, stresss, and happiness. 

**Key findings** from combining both overall_analysis.csv and sentiment_analysis.csv
1) Penn: Penn had a better financial outcome with a stronger ROI and lower debt-to-income, but a more stressful undergraduate experience.
2) UIUC: UIUC appeared to have a more positive undergraduate experience driven by a community sentiment, but faltered in long-term financial return. However, UIUC is a strong force in broad economic mobility.  

**Business Schools Folder:** It includes three datasets. One on full-time position percentage data for Wharton and two datasets comparing mean starting salaries for both Wharton and Gies' Accounting, Finance, and Marketing majors across 2021-2025.

All datasets were analyzed in a similar way: visually graphing the trends in income for both schools and comparing and contrasting across majors. While Wharton clearly has the edge in starting salary, Gies is slowly moving up in all majors while some Wharton majors are decreasing in salary or hitting a plateau (accounting and finance respectively). This is very interesting to see given that Gies is known for their accounting program. 

In the Business School ROI notebook, there is comparision of the salary progressions of both Gies and Wharton's top industry placement: accounting and investment banking respectively and see when they "break even" given their 4 year costs. From the graphs, it is clear Wharton has a stronger and earlier ROI with ultimate higher salaries pursuing IB. We also looked at the overall industry placements and how they relate to career culture at each college.

Using career reports, this section compared average starting salaries, longitudinal salary trends across accounting, finance, and marketing concentrations, industry placement distributions, and ROI in post-graduate placements like Big Four accounting for Gies and Investment banking for Wharton. It also looked into financial aid accessibility across income brackets. 

**Key findings** 
1) Wharton: Higher average starting salaries and elite finance pipelines, but outcomes reflect recruiting culture as much as academics. 
2) Gles: Greater industry diversity, a rising accounting trajectory, and for low-income studets, has potentially lower net cost.

**Engineering Folder:** 
This section compares two engineering schools (Penn SEAS vs Grainger). The analysis examines median and average starting salaries, post-graduation outcome structures (full-time employment vs further education post undergrad), industry and career pathway distributions, and an ROI breakeven estimate using net cost differentials against the median salary gap. Data used was from career outcome reports and supplemented contextual research. 

**Key findings** 
1) Penn SEAS: Higher median starting salary and faster market entry. This school is driven by a dynamic pipeline of using engineering into finance and consulting alongside tech.
2) UIUC's Grainger: Stronger pure engineering reputation (tehnical skills), but a large share of graduates pursue advanced degrees, delaying immediate earnings.
